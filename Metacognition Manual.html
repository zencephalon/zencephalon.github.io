This treatise is my attempt to share the joy of meta-cognition with you. Using meta-cognition I cured myself of depression and attention deficit disorder by gaining control over my emotions and thoughts. Stages 1 and 2 explain how I did this.

I also learned to rest my mind in states of enormous bliss. I haven't mastered this sufficiently to teach it yet (I've only been meditating for a year), but I will return to this when I have. Stage 3 will explain how to empty your mind and improve your concentration so you can explore for the samadhi states yourself. They may arise naturally, and there are many books you can refer to. I will record a list of my recommendations after reviewing the books I've read to determine which were of genuine benefit.

Meta-cognition is the process of mind thinking about mind. This self awareness is our distinguishing trait, and by gaining mastery over the brain to such an extent that we can dynamically reprogram it we reap benefits I'm only beginning to discover. Realize that you are not your thoughts, just as you are not your body, and step out of your thoughts to command them for your benefit.

This is a guide to meditation, but you'll notice I do not mention sitting. Meditation is portable, and you will not benefit fully from it until you apply it throughout daily life.

Stage 1: Normal Consciousness Diagnosis: Many thoughts escape your inspection. Remedy: Noting practice.

At this stage we are like most people. We may occasionally meta-cognate, that is, think about thoughts, but the majority of our thoughts are not inspected.

Negative thoughts such as hatred rule us through our tacit consent. Unless we notice we are thinking a hateful thought and consciously counteract it, then we allow it to control our actions (we become angry).

If you've ever been negatively affected by acting out of anger, sadness, lust, or fear then you can imagine how mastering these emotions would be beneficial.

Our strategy at this stage is to become aware of every single thought passing through our minds, giving each one a close inspection. This can be accomplished through noting practice.

Noting ------ Try to attach a short mental label to each distinct sensation that arises. For example, 'body' for any bodily sensation. 'Thought' for any thoughts. 'In' for the in-breath. 'Out' for the out-breath. Begin by trying to note as many different sensations as possible per second. When you can comfortably note ten sensations per second, then improve the accuracy of your labels. Divide your thoughts into categories such as 'planning', 'fantasizing', 'remembering', etc. Pinpoint bodily sensations to their origin. For example, 'left arm', 'right ring finger', 'left thigh', 'heart', 'belly', etc.

Practice noting until it becomes automatic. When you have done this you will have vastly increased your conscious awareness. Nothing that happens to you will escape your notice. This comes as both a blessing and a curse, as you will become acutely aware of much negativity. Thus we continue to the next stage.

Stage 2: Awareness without Control Diagnosis: You are aware of your thoughts, but unable to control them. Remedy: Establish the breath anchor.

Now that we are aware of our thoughts we notice many we don't want. How do we banish them? If you've ever tried not thinking about orange unicorns you might be familiar with the challenge we face. Any way we think of not wanting to think about orange unicorns causes us to think about orange unicorns more.

To stop thinking about orange unicorns think about something else to such a degree that you forget all about orange unicorns. To rid ourselves of a thought we need something else to latch onto.

When we want to stop a thought short, we move our awareness to the breath. Why use our breath? When we are having a particularly bad day our worrying thoughts will repeatedly draw us in. Thus we need a continual reminder to "stop worrying, live in the moment". We can use the rhythm of our breath as that trigger. Each time we breath out remember "don't worry, just breath".

Our breath and our emotions are linked. When we are stressed we breath short and shallow. When we are relaxed we breath long and deep. That's how we always want to breath, to ensure our brains get enough oxygen. If you control your breathing so that it is deep you will automatically be relaxed.

In order to gain mastery over our thoughts and emotions we establish the breath as the foundation of our awareness. Consider the breath your mental home base. Imagine that every time your thoughts stray from your breath it's like exploring the countryside outside your castle. You will encounter many pleasant thoughts out there. Whenever you encounter a thought that is useless to you, such as ones of anger or depression, you just stroll back to your castle.

When we first begin this practice negative thoughts will keep arising. Our goal at this point isn't to stop those thoughts from arising at all. It's just to cut them off once we notice them by returning to breath. Instead of thinking "Man, I really hate Suzy. She's such a bitch." think "Man, I really ha--" and get back to breathing! Over time our brains rewire and those negative thoughts come less and less, until one day you notice you've thought nothing but nice thoughts about other people.

Breathing --------- Breath with your diaphragm. The easy way to do this is imagine breathing into your belly. When you breath in, your belly expands, because your diaphragm lowers. When you breath out, your belly compress, pushing your diaphragm up. Breathing this way gives us our full lung capacity, unlike chest breathing.
